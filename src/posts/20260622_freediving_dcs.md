Historically when people have asked me this question, my answer has been: no. I was wrong. 

In my head, the bends was a sickness that occurred as a result of breathing underwater. Although I’ve never personally taken a scuba class, exposure through the diving community has taught me that there’s a lot of instruction around decompression sickness (DCS). So, when devices like the lungtank or scorkl were released in the last few years, I was pretty alarmed that we were just putting these devices in the hands of people who had done no training and did not have the understanding of even amateur scuba divers of what causes the bends and how to avoid it. Since we don’t breathe underwater, freediving was much safer than these devices, and I was Better Than Them™.

(Turns out that although DCS is a risk, the bigger risk for these devices is lung overexpansion. But that’s beside the point.)

As a freediver who thought the bends didn’t apply to me, it was almost by chance that I decided to look up what actually causes the phenomenon.

As we dive deeper, the pressure of the air and water above us increases significantly. Under this much pressure, some of the nitrogen gas in our body will start to dissolve into our bloodstream and tissues. Especially as divers spend long stretches of time underwater breathing from a tank (which, like the air we typically breathe, contain large percentages of nitrogen), a significant amount of nitrogen can accumulate over that time.

The problem arises when divers ascend too quickly. Upon the release of pressure, the nitrogen wants to escape all at once, and this results in the creation of bubbles in the blood vessels and tissues, which can cause ruptures of blood vessels, annurisms, and more. Ascending slowly with stops allows the gas to release slowly through exhalation, and mitigates the risk of nitrogen bubbles building up in the blood. 

Okay but one of the key things for this is that nitrogen in the scuba tank, plus the extended amount of time underwater, are what really make this a major risk. How can freedivers, who do not breathe underwater and are under pressure for less than five minutes, get DCS?

Basically what can happen is that when we descend, our tissues and blood vessels will accumulate some small amount of nitrogen. This won’t affect freedivers on their first descent, but if we continue to dive over and over, and the nitrogen has no chance to fully exit our body, then the levels will eventually become high enough that the bubbles form upon ascention. The problem is compounded when divers take shorter surface breaks (which don’t allow the nitrogen from the last dive to disperse) or if we are diving deeper (which creates more nitrogen buildup).[^1]

When completing my AIDA2 certification, something that was stressed was waiting on the surface for several minutes in between each dive. Turns out the reason is to release some of that nitrogen accumulation and avoid DCS. Luckily, it’s very avoidable and incredibly rare, and the rule that once seemed arbitrary finally makes sense.

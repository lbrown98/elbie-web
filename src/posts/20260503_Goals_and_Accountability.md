I returned from Taiwan with goals. Things I wanted to accomplish and ways I wanted to improve in order to take advantage of the stable environment I would be in for the next several years. Instead of letting life happen to me, I wanted to chase it and actively play a part in building my own future. 

I began by loosely working on making the US feel like home, and by New Year’s 2024 I was ready to focus. Around that time, I adopted two new strategies that changed how I set and track goals, and that I still use today.

### The Yearly Theme

The yearly theme was directly inspired by [CGP Grey’s six minute video on the topic](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NVGuFdX5guE), which was sent to me by my good friend Chris. Grey’s argument is that committing to a specific resolution like “I want to ride my bike three times per week,” feels like an impossible task to maintain, especially after week four, when you might ride only \*gasp\* twice instead of three times, and then you’ve broken your resolution and are clearly a failure, so why continue to keep up with it anyway. Instead, commit to a yearly theme, and keep that theme in mind as you make decisions throughout the year. 

For example: year of health. This was my yearly theme in 2024, and I spent the year ensuring that I addressed any health issues I had hitherto been ignoring. And although I didn’t focus on it that year, you can also include “health” to mean that you want to work out more consistently or eat more healthily. Your theme is whatever it means to you, and can even change over time.

What matters for life improvement aren’t exact data points, but the trend. If the trend is going up, you’re improving. If you slow or halt a negative trend, you’re also improving! The yearly theme reframes failure, so instead of thinking poorly of yourself for not hitting the gym 3x this week, it allows you to appreciate that you went at all (which is better than last year, when you spent the whole year on your couch eating potato chips). Or, if you didn’t go this week because you had a work trip or were feeling particularly down, you still focused on eating healthily. Every day is a new opportunity to recommit to the theme, since you’re going for the overall trend rather than rigid numeric goals.

My theme this year is trust. I’m working on trusting myself by keeping promises I make to myself, and by teaching myself that I can do hard things.

### Goal Bingo

Think of 24-25 things that would be nice to accomplish this year. Some of these goals should be relatively easy, like “go to a movie in theaters” or “read one book”, and some should be harder, like “move to a new city” or “try solo traveling for the first time.” Your goals should be things that you want to do but haven’t prioritized. They don’t all have to be incredibly realistic; for example, I wrote down “bike the whole C&O canal” in 2024, though I hadn’t sat on a bicycle in years. Throw them on a bingo card and check them off as you achieve them.  

Maybe you’ll accomplish everything, maybe not. But accomplishing everything isn’t the goal of the bingo card; _getting bingo is_. And even if you don’t, progress in itself is still a victory. The card that you’ve created helps guide your path towards that victory. 

To make it harder for myself, I design the card so that the placement of each goal isn’t random; if accomplishing one goal will easily lead into accomplishing another, I place these two on the board in such a way that achieving both won’t double-count for my progress towards bingo. I also place something particularly difficult in the center (rather than a free space, I have to really work for this crossroad), and balance out the difficulty of each row.

### Bonus: Solo Sprints

Sitting in the West Virginia snow in mid-February, I realized that even though I had my theme and my goals set for the year, I didn’t really have a way to track my progress or hold myself accountable. As I contemplated this issue, I kept coming back to [Agile](https://www.atlassian.com/agile). Setting a few goals for each sprint enables my team at work to stay on track for larger milestones. Why not implement something similar in my own life?

I started scheduling 30 minutes at the beginning of each month and quarter, and 15 minutes at the end of each week. Each meeting, I:
- Reflect on the goals I set the last meeting. What did I accomplish? What did I struggle or fail at, and why? How can I improve?
- Consider what I need to do to stay on target for the goals I set for the year/quarter/month/etc; and
- Set new goals to work on until the next meeting

As an example, let’s back up and pretend it's April. The first quarter has just passed, and I’ve made progress on several of my bingo card goals. Even better, I’ve checked off two! 

Although I’ve been making progress in my commitment to kayak a class 4 rapid, I’ve let it become an excuse to neglect my pistol squat. Reflecting on my priorities, I decide to focus on kayaking while it’s warm outside, and leave the pistol squat as a goal for the late fall and winter. I intended to book that solo trip last quarter, but war and a corresponding increase in gas prices have made that financially unrealistic. I don’t want to de-prioritize this one, but I decide to look for flights for later in the year. 

Looking at my bingo card, what I can work on next is:
- 3m breath hold
- Doubling my emergency fund 
- Make a personal website

Luckily for me, April is a three paycheck month, which will make the goal of doubling my emergency fund much more achievable. Getting my breath hold to three minutes will be a bit harder, so I resolve to start a month-long breath hold challenge to get as close as I can (looking at you, May!). The last thing on this list is making a personal website, and I set aside some time in April to work on it. 

Now it’s May. Although I haven’t quite doubled it yet, I’ve made serious progress with my emergency fund, and have started that month-long challenge to improve my breath hold (I’m keeping myself accountable here by aiming to make a YouTube video about it). For the website, look around! I checked that one off my card a couple weeks ago. I use my monthly meeting to re-commit to what I want to work on this month: kayaking, freediving, and saving. 

My weekly meetings are scheduled on Sundays (today!). Last week, I spent more than I intended on eating out, and also used the recent chill as an excuse not to be on the water. Rather than dwelling on the past, I treat this time as a reset. I’m spending time today meal planning for the upcoming week, and I’ve scheduled kayaking for Monday and Wednesday after work, as well as for Friday morning with some friends. And don’t forget my daily breath hold practice, with a check-in on Thursday during my diving club meetup. 

Looking back across the last few months, this strategy has helped me stay accountable to myself and to my goals more than anything else I’ve tried. And nothing builds trust like keeping promises I’ve made to myself.

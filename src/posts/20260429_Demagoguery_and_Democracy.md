Welcome, everyone, to my first book review. Today (and for the past several weeks, only this time in writing!), I'm examining the book Demagoguery and Democracy by Patricia Roberts-Miller. This book explores what demagoguery is, why it’s dangerous, and attempts to give some tips on how we can reverse it. In this review, I’m going to go through some of the major themes that really stood out and spoke to me, make some connections with our current global political climate, and explain how this book and its contents have affected me on a personal level. Let’s get started!

### Naïve Realism, Relativism, and The Truth™
> “If we want to think of ourselves as people with good judgement, or if ambiguity and complexity makes us uncomfortable, it will be painful to look back at decisions and acknowledge our own participation in the mistake. We will face substantial cognitive dissonance between our view of ourselves as good people with good judgement and an assessment of our past actions that acknowledges the mistakes. Demagoguery helps resolve that cognitive dissonance by telling us that we can stop questioning ourselves and our judgement.” (26-27)

> “What criticizing naive realism does suggest, and the reason that criticism is terrifying for some people, is that feeling certain is not the same thing as being right.” (44)

After starting off by explaining what demagoguery is not, the author introduces this concept of “naïve realism.” A naïve realist is someone who tries to interpret the truth of the world by simply perceiving it, and believes that their perception of the world is objective. This way of thinking “increases the tendency towards confirmation bias (since people tend to perceive more easily and quickly any information that confirms their current beliefs)” (40). They rely on binaries that reinforce naïve realism, and tend to think that something is either True or False. On the other hand, a relativist is someone who believes that there is no Truth, or rather that any Truth that exists is relative to a person, a situation, or a society. Citing our ability to make decisions in probabilistic situations like “will it rain today,” Roberts-Miller maintains that “the binary between naïve realism and relativism is false” (42).

For the past year, my brother and I have been debating morality and the meaning of life. He has, not unlovingly, called me a nihilist. Although I generally object on the grounds of existentialism, it’s true that for the last unknown period of time, I’ve believed that there is no inherent purpose to life, and that any purpose we have, we make for ourselves. This train of thought, for me, extends to relativism; with no clear end goal for life or humanity, and with people making their own meaning for themselves, how can we judge the choices and moral codes people make for themselves in order to escape the Unbearable Lightness of Being? (Leaving my full thoughts on that book for a later date.)

That being said, I hate this way of thinking, but can’t find my way out of it. Life is complicated!! Our world is complicated! People grow up in different environments, raised to believe in and value different things. But it’s vertigo-inducing, not having a clear view of right and wrong. Making it worse is the fact that everyone around me seems so certain of the difference between the two, though nobody can agree on what goes into each category. 

How can people be so certain of themselves? Have they done all the research to determine what they really believe? How do they find the time?!

Needless to say, all of this has been causing me a lot of angst the last few months. This book has offered a degree of relief I can’t even begin to describe. It has helped me realize that most people don’t do the research. From personal experience, not knowing is incredibly uncomfortable. By handing over the knowledge to the people we as a society assume are doing the research, it allows us to turn the heavy, democratic responsibility of knowing over to our representatives. I’m going to go into this more in the next section, but understanding this helps me to release somewhat the pressure I have been putting on myself to make a decision. It also helped understand that, just because I recognize that the world is complicated, that does not necessarily make me a relativist, and that there is a middle ground out there for me to explore.


### Charismatic leadership and an “escape from freedom”
One aspect of an increasingly demagogic society is the rise of charismatic leaders. This is not the business definition of charismatic leaders; not “a leader who is inspirational and thereby gets a greater level of commitment to a vision” (57). A charismatic leader is a person who has “arouse[d] [...] belief in himself or herself as the source of legitimacy” (58), and has four characteristics:

> - The leader is perceived by the followers as somehow superhuman.
> - The followers blindly believe the leader’s statements.
> - The followers unconditionally comply with the leader’s directives for action.
> - The followers give the leader unqualified emotional commitment.[^1]

Charismatic leadership strikes me as demagoguery personified. Making decisions is difficult. On the other hand, the sheer number of options we have is overwhelming; in the words of Søren Kierkegaard, “anxiety is the dizziness of freedom,”[^2] and the possibility of being wrong is terrifying for many on several levels. By handing over the responsibility of making a decision to a leader or political party, we relieve both the lightness and dizziness of the responsibility to make a decision and the responsibility of being wrong (and, by extension, learning from our mistakes). 

A major consequence of this turning over this responsibility to our leaders is that “our sense of self-worth gets attached to the identity of the leader, so that we take personally any criticism of the leader, and have as much difficulty admitting flaws or errors on the leader’s part as we do on our own” (59). I’ve felt for a while that it’s impossible to criticize the viewpoint of your in-group political party in modern western society. Holding or expressing any view that deviates from that of the prevailing party is taken by peers as a personal betrayal, and friendships end over relatively minor differences in opinion. 

As a result, I have felt almost unfree to think critically about political issues, fearing that I’ll be discarded by my friends if the conclusions I come to differ from those they have reached, or those that their party promotes. This, at the same time that I’ve simultaneously felt an immense amount of self-imposed pressure to form an opinion.

### The snowball effect of profit-driven media
> “For people competing for media markets, consumers, voters, and so on, demagoguery is likely to be the more effective rhetorical strategy, and more rhetors will choose it. And rhetors have to out-demagogue each other to get attention, buyers, voters.” (79)

> “The point of such public discourse is not to find the best solution to a community’s problems, but to triumph over the other side.” (pg 84) 

> "Television programs that offer nuanced analyses can’t compete with programs that simultaneously enrage and please; talk radio that is fair to all positions is too cognitively complex to be engaging; and print or online media that says they might have a point is frustrating." (89)

> "When it’s for profit, then the goal is [...] to hold an audience, which necessarily means trying to persuade the audience that they don’t need to look at other sources." (89)

I think the author explained this part of the book so perfectly, in words that I have no hope of competing with. Demagoguery feeds on itself. In the modern day, people are shouting into echo chambers, and, hearing their opinions confirmed by the echo, nod in agreement and affirm their views as “correct.” 

Further, echo chambers can create alternative realities, in which people who marinate in them are presented with curated versions of events, or even with stories of events that simply never happened. They hear watered-down or completely false versions of what the other side thinks of an issue, and then pat themselves on the back for being open minded and entertaining “alternative perspectives.” 

It seems that objective facts no longer exist in the media. If you want to find out what really happened, you have to spend a non-trivial amount of time researching on websites that tout opposing viewpoints, and read between the lines to find the middle ground. By the way, you’re expected to juggle this research in between work, a social life, a relationship, activities, cooking, eating, and getting adequate rest. 

And all the while, my deep distrust of modern media has further compounded the paralysis I’ve been experiencing re: forming independent opinions.

Let's take a moment here to lament the repeal of the [FCC Fairness Doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairness_doctrine). Society needs you, now more than ever. Sigh.

### Fighting demagoguery
Ok so by this point in the book, we’ve established what demagoguery is, what it’s not, and why it’s dangerous. How do we fight it? She offers four easy steps:
Reduce the profitability of demagoguery by not consuming it, and shaming media outlets who rely on it
Try to persuade people who are repeating demagogic talking points, but don’t get sucked into arguments
Argue with people who are repeating demagogic talking points (she introduces and explains a lot of fallacies in this section that the reader can use to argue with people over) 
Support and fight for democratic deliberation

An overarching theme of this section is: we fight through education and empathy. To quote the book yet again, “demagoguery about them is undone by empathy” (pg 99). If you can find a way to help others see things from the perspective of their enemies (for lack of a better word), it’s a key way to help people realize that issues are more complicated than they initially realize, and their conclusion isn’t the obvious conclusion. 

While I don’t disagree with these points, this conclusion lends itself to considerable despair because: how the heck are we going to impose empathy for the out-group en masse? This strategy feels similar to: in order to pursue the goal of reducing our carbon footprint, you as an individual should cease to eat meat. If every individual decided to become vegetarian, yeah there’d be a massive positive impact on global GHG emissions, but the vast majority of people will not voluntarily decide to become vegetarian. Even if it’s not good for our planet, people like meat! 

Similarly, demagoguery doesn’t exist because it’s good for society, it exists because making decisions is hard, admitting that you could be wrong is scary, and the little hits of dopamine when people’s views are validated is nice. What we need is organizational change, societal change. How do we create that? Even if I as an individual decide to argue with all my friends and family, try to help them be empathetic towards people they believe that they potentially believe are stupid and/or evil, and risk complete alienation from the people I love, I will still be one person fighting against an ocean of systems that profit from division. 

Or maybe I’ll find other vegetarians. Who knows.

### Overall
This book was informative, important, and very well written. The author did a great job of remaining politically impartial by using examples from history rather than relying on those from modern-day. It’s short and succinct, and explains concepts thoroughly by using examples without being long-winded. It helped me internalize that complexity is the natural way of things; the facade of certainty that a lot of people put on is a result of demagoguery, or it’s genuinely just a reflection of their ability to make decisions in the face of uncertainty. 

Highly recommend 10/10. Everyone should read this.

### Extra credit: 2026/04/21 VA redistricting vote 
> “Demagoguery [...] presents the situation of the in-group as so dire that we are justified in any actions, deeming values such as fairness across groups [...] unnecessary and possibly suicidal.” (pg 34-35)

In case you’re somehow not tired from reading this very long book review, here’s a fun fact: I finished reading this book during the period of time before the 2026 midterm elections where states across the country were trying to emergency-gerrymander their districts in an attempt to gain more seats in Congress. The background is that: Trump encouraged Texas to redistrict, and they did. Then California got pissed and retaliated by also redistricting, and then it just cascaded from there. 

Before understanding the context, I read the VA special election to “redistrict” as a scummy gerrymandering scheme. Then I was informed that it was an attempt to “cancel out other gerrymandering,” which makes the situation more complicated. It’s always more complicated. 

My principles are incredibly anti-gerrymandering. At the same time, with all the changes that have happened in our country over the last couple years, it’s genuinely scary to think about what might be possible with a Republican controlled Congress. And how much of my perceived reality is real vs what’s being sold to me by the echo chamber I’m floating in?

In the end, the “yes” vote won, with 51.7% of the state voting for redistricting and 48.3% voting against it. It feels wrong that less than 104k votes should decide the fate of almost 8.9 million voters. Does the potential for what Trump might do with a Republican controlled congress justify silencing all those voices? To me, the answer is not obvious.

Did I make the right decision? History will tell. At least I thought through it.
